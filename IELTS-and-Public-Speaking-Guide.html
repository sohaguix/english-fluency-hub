      Two goals, one skill

      IELTS Speaking is scored on fluency, coherence, vocabulary, grammar, and pronunciation — the exact qualities that make you good in a meeting or a conversation. So you won't study "for the exam." You'll build habits that make you a better speaker, and the band score follows.

      Rule 01

      Consistency beats intensity

      Twenty focused minutes every day beats three hours on Sunday. Your mouth and brain need frequent reps, not occasional marathons.

      Rule 02

      Speak out loud, daily

      Reading and listening are input. Speaking is the skill being tested and the one you actually want. Silent study never builds a speaking muscle.

      Rule 03

      Record yourself

      The highest-value habit here. You can't hear your own mistakes in the moment. Your phone's voice recorder is your most honest teacher.

      How the IELTS speaking test works

      Part 1 is a short interview about familiar topics (home, work, hobbies). Part 2 is the "long turn" — a cue card, one minute to prepare, then you speak alone for one to two minutes. Part 3 is a deeper two-way discussion of abstract ideas linked to Part 2.

      You're scored on four things: Fluency & Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range & Accuracy, and Pronunciation. Keep those four in mind — they're also exactly what makes anyone pleasant to listen to.

      Section 01

      Daily speaking exercises

      Pick the block that fits your day. Each is self-contained — rotate them so you don't get bored. Every session runs the same loop: prepare briefly, speak out loud, record, listen back, fix one thing.

      The 10-minute minimum

      busy days · 10 min
      0–2 MIN
      Warm up the mouth. Read any paragraph aloud, slowly, over-articulating every sound. Tongue twisters count.
      2–7 MIN
      Talk about your day. Narrate what you did and what's next, in full sentences. Record it.
      7–10 MIN
      Listen back. Note one thing to fix tomorrow — a repeated filler, a grammar slip, a mumbled word.

      The 20-minute core routine

      your default · 20 min
      0–3 MIN
      Shadowing. Play 60–90 sec of a native speaker and speak along, matching rhythm and intonation.
      3–8 MIN
      Cue-card long turn. Random topic, one minute prep, speak for two. Record it.
      8–13 MIN
      One conversation topic. Answer 3–4 questions on it aloud, in full.
      13–18 MIN
      Vocabulary in action. Take 3 new phrases and force them into sentences about your own life.
      18–20 MIN
      Review the recording. One win, one fix.

      The 30-minute deep session

      when you have time · 30 min
      CORE
      Everything in the 20-minute routine, then add the two blocks below.
      +5 MIN
      Impromptu speaking. Random topic, zero prep, speak for 90 seconds.
      +5 MIN
      Pronunciation drill. Target one sound or one intonation pattern.
      The golden loop

      Prepare briefly → speak out loud → record → listen back → fix one thing. Do this loop daily and you'll improve whether or not you do anything else in this guide.

      The PEEL structure

      Point (your answer) → Explain (why) → Example (a specific instance) → Link (wrap up). One structure that fixes the two biggest problems at once: answers that are too short, and answers that ramble with no direction.

      Section 03

      Everyday vocabulary & phrases

      Don't memorize word lists. Learn chunks you can drop into speech, and use each one out loud the same day. A phrase you've spoken ten times is yours; a phrase you've read once is not.

      Giving opinions

      If you ask me…The way I see it…I'd say…It depends on…I'm a big fan of…I'm not really into…

      Agreeing & disagreeing, politely

      That's a fair point.I see where you're coming from.I get what you mean, but…I'm not so sure about that.Couldn't agree more.

      Buying thinking time

      Use these instead of "um" and long silences — natives do this constantly. They keep you fluent while your brain catches up.

      That's a good question, let me think…Off the top of my head…Now that you mention it…How can I put this…

      Vivid description — lifts your Lexical Resource

      Retire "good / bad / very tired / nice place."

      brilliantsolidtop-notchimpressiveawfuldisappointingmediocreexhausted · drainedcharmingcozybuzzingpeaceful

      Linking words — raise your Coherence

      On top of that…What's more…Then again…Having said that…As a result…Which is why…For instance…All in all…At the end of the day…

      Professional phrases

      Just to circle back on…To give you some context…I'd like to add something here.Let me clarify.That's outside my area, but I can find out.
      Section 04

      Grammar fixes that matter most

      You don't need perfect grammar — you need a range of structures used mostly accurately. Don't drill these as a list. Hunt for them in your own recordings, then say the corrected sentence aloud three times.

      01Missing or wrong articles (a / an / the)
      ✗I am student. · I went to gym.
      ✓I am a student. · I went to the gym.
      Singular countable nouns almost always need an article.
      02Third-person "-s" dropped
      ✗She work in a bank. · It take time.
      ✓She works in a bank. · It takes time.
      03Wrong or inconsistent tense
      ✗Yesterday I go to the market.
      ✓Yesterday I went to the market.
      Watch the jump between past and present mid-story — pick a tense and stay in it.
      04Present perfect vs. past simple
      ✓I have lived here for five years. (still true now)
      ✓I lived there in 2019. (finished past)
      05Prepositions — learn as chunks
      ✓good at, interested in, depends on, arrive at a place / in a city, on Monday, in July, at 6pm
      06Countable vs. uncountable nouns
      ✗many informations · advices · furnitures
      ✓much information, some advice, a piece of furniture (no plural)
      07Double subjects
      ✗My brother he is a doctor.
      ✓My brother is a doctor.
      08Adjective / adverb mix-ups
      ✗She speaks English very good.
      ✓She speaks English very well.
      09"Since" vs. "for"
      ✓for three years (duration) · since 2021 (starting point)
      10Word order in questions
      ✗You know where is the station?
      ✓Do you know where the station is? (embedded questions keep normal word order)
      Section 05

      Pronunciation & fluency

      The goal is clarity, not a native accent — IELTS requires no particular accent. The fastest single upgrade: slow down slightly and finish your word endings.

      Sound-level drills

      Get the sounds right

      • Minimal pairs: ship/sheep, bit/beat, full/fool, three/tree, very/berry. Exaggerate the difference.
      • The "th" sounds: tongue lightly between teeth — think, this, weather, mouth.
      • Word endings: over-pronounce them — asked, wants, helped, next. A dropped "-ed" or "-s" loses meaning and grammar marks.
      Rhythm & stress · the real key

      Sound natural, not robotic

      • Word stress: pho-TO-gra-phy, com-FOR-ta-ble, e-CO-no-my. Wrong stress = "I can't understand you."
      • Sentence stress: hit the content words — "I want to go to the beach tomorrow." Tap the table on each.
      • Linking: "an apple" → "a-napple"; "turn it off" → "tur-ni-toff."
      • Intonation: voice rises on yes/no questions, falls on statements. Flat = unconfident.
      Fluency drill

      The 4 / 3 / 2

      Speak on one topic for 4 minutes, then the same topic in 3, then in 2. Same content, less time — forces smoother, faster delivery. It works fast.

      Fluency drill

      No-filler challenge

      Record 90 seconds, count your "um / uh / like." Next attempt, replace each with a short silence or a thinking phrase. Silence sounds more confident than "ummm."

      Fluency drill

      Read aloud daily

      Five minutes reading any text aloud, smoothly, builds mouth stamina and surfaces words you can't yet pronounce.

      Section 06

      Public speaking techniques

      Same mechanics whether it's a presentation, a meeting, an IELTS long turn, or a wedding toast: manage your body, voice, and nerves so the message lands.

      Confidence

      Nerves are fuel

      • Preparation is 80% of confidence. Always over-prepare your first 20 seconds.
      • Reframe it: the racing heart is the same state as excitement. Tell yourself "I'm excited."
      • Power pose two minutes before, in private.
      • Accept imperfection. Recovering smoothly beats never slipping.
      Body language

      Open & still

      • Stand or sit tall — open posture signals confidence to the room and to your own brain.
      • Open hands, purposeful gestures. Let hands illustrate; don't restrain natural movement.
      • Plant your feet — no swaying or pacing. Stillness reads as authority.
      • Smile when it fits.
      Voice

      Project & vary

      • Volume: slightly louder than feels natural. Under-projecting reads as low confidence.
      • Variety: vary pitch and pace so you don't drone.
      • Pauses: the most underused tool. Pause before a key point and after it.
      • Warm up — hum or read aloud for a minute first.
      Pacing & eye contact

      Slow down, connect

      • Slow down. Nervous speakers rush; a deliberate pace sounds confident and buys thinking time.
      • Chunk your speech into short phrases with small pauses. Breathe at natural points.
      • Talk to individuals — hold eye contact for a full thought (3–5 sec), then move on. Sweeping connects with no one.
      • Practicing alone? Speak to your webcam or sticky-note "faces" at eye level.
      Section 07

      Storytelling & impromptu speaking

      Storytelling makes you engaging; impromptu skill makes you unshakeable. Both are trainable — the fear of "I won't know what to say" disappears once you have a structure to fall back on.

      The story spine

      Four parts, under two minutes

      • Setup — who, where, when. "Last year, I was starting a new job…"
      • Tension — the problem or turning point. "…and on day one, everything went wrong."
      • Resolution — what happened / what you did.
      • Point — the lesson, or why you told it.

      Add sensory detail — "it was pouring rain," "my hands were shaking." Specifics make stories vivid and show richer vocabulary.

      Impromptu frameworks

      Grab a frame, fill it in

      • PREP — Point → Reason → Example → Point. Best all-purpose frame.
      • Past / Present / Future — how it was, is, will be. Great for any "change" topic.
      • Three reasons — "First… Second… Finally…" Instant structure and time.
      • On one hand / on the other — for any "do you agree?" question.
      The rescue rule

      If you blank, don't apologize or freeze. Buy time — "That's an interesting question, let me think…" — restate the question in your own words, and start with a framework. Momentum returns once you're moving. Daily drill: draw a random topic, breathe, and speak for 60–90 seconds using a frame. Within two weeks the panic fades.

      Section 08

      Questions to answer aloud daily

      Answer a handful out loud, in full sentences, every day. Record at least one. Rotation: 3 warm-ups + 1 long-turn daily; add 1–2 deep-dives on longer sessions.

      Warm-up · Part 1 style

      Quick & familiar

      • What did you do this morning?
      • Your favorite way to relax, and why?
      • Describe your hometown in three sentences.
      • Mornings or evenings? Why?
      • What are you looking forward to?
      Long-turn · Part 2 style

      Speak for 1–2 minutes

      • Describe a person who influenced you.
      • Describe a place you'd love to visit.
      • Describe a skill you learned recently.
      • A decision you're glad you made.
      • A book, film, or show that stayed with you.
      Deep-dive · Part 3 style

      Opinions & abstract

      • Is technology making us more or less connected?
      • Passion or a stable career?
      • How has your city changed in ten years?
      • One thing schools should teach but don't?
      • Is remote work good for society?
      Reflection · think in English

      Narrate these in your head

      • What went well today, and what would I change?
      • A problem I'm facing — what are my options?
      • How would I explain my job to a child?
      Section 09

      Shadowing & listening

      Shadowing — speaking along with a native speaker, copying their exact sounds and melody, like singing along to a song — is the highest-leverage technique for pronunciation, rhythm, and fluency all at once.

      How to shadow

      60–90 sec clips · 5–10 min/day
      STEP 1
      Listen to a short clip once, just to understand it.
      STEP 2
      Read along with the transcript or subtitles while it plays.
      STEP 3
      Shadow simultaneously — speak at the same time, matching rhythm and intonation. You'll stumble; that's the point.
      STEP 4
      Shadow again without looking at the text.
      STEP 5
      Record yourself shadowing and compare to the original.
      Train your ear

      So fast speech stops scaring you

      • Active listening: after a 3–5 min clip, say a spoken summary of what you heard.
      • The 80% rule: pick material you understand ~80% of — hard enough to learn, easy enough to follow.
      • Dictation: play a sentence, write exactly what you heard, check. Brutal but sharpening.
      Immersion

      Fill the day with English

      • Slow-then-normal: play a tough clip at 0.75×, understand it, replay at normal speed — it'll feel easy.
      • Passive immersion: podcasts or radio on while cooking, commuting, cleaning. Soaks your ear in natural rhythm.
      Section 10

      Thinking directly in English

      Translating in your head is the biggest bottleneck to fluency — slow, and it produces awkward, first-language-shaped sentences. Build a direct link between ideas and English with daily habits.

      • Narrate your life in your head, in English — all day. "I'm making tea. It's a bit cold. I should reply to that." This low-pressure habit rewires the reflex more than any lesson.
      • Name objects around you instantly, without translating. A word you don't know is a vocabulary lesson.
      • Think in whole phrases, not single words — reuse chunks so you're not building every sentence from scratch.
      • Talk around missing words instead of freezing to translate. "The thing you use to open bottles." Strong candidates do exactly this.
      • Keep an English-only journal — 2–3 sentences a day, written straight in English.
      • Switch your phone to English, and feed your brain English right before bed. Immersion sticks better as the last input of the day.
      The mindset

      Accept simple but direct over complex but translated. A plain English sentence you built directly is worth far more than an elaborate one assembled from your first language. Simplicity now leads to fluency later.

      Section 11

      Weekly confidence challenges

      One per week, escalating. Each pushes you slightly out of your comfort zone — because confidence isn't a feeling you wait for, it's a byproduct of doing the scary thing and surviving.

      Week 1

      Record & rewatch

      Record a 2-minute video answering a topic. Watch it fully — uncomfortable but essential. You're building tolerance for seeing and hearing yourself.

      Week 2

      One real conversation

      Have at least one genuine spoken exchange in English — a language partner, a colleague, a shopkeeper, an AI voice tutor. Initiate it.

      Week 3

      Teach something

      Explain a topic you know well, out loud, for three minutes, as if teaching a class. Teaching forces clear structure and confident delivery.

      Week 4

      Speak to an audience

      Present for 3–5 minutes to at least one real person and take a question afterward. Live audience, live nerves, real growth.

      Ongoing stretch goals: join an online speaking group or a local Toastmasters club, post a short spoken video publicly, volunteer to speak first in a meeting, or record a weekly "spoken diary."

      Treat AI band scores as useful estimates, not official marks — they're directional. AI is excellent for volume, instant feedback, and zero scheduling, but pair it with at least one real human conversation a week. The pressure and unpredictability of a real person is exactly what you're training for, and it's the one thing AI still can't fully reproduce.
